# AI in Actuarial Science

## Where AI Appears

Actuarial science centers on estimating risk over time. The work often ties to insurance claims, pricing decisions, or long-term financial obligations. AI surfaces when large volumes of historical data inform those estimates and when models need to account for complexity at scale.

Students typically encounter these ideas while building and evaluating models. Uncertainty and assumptions are part of the work itself. AI is introduced alongside traditional actuarial methods as one more way of handling complex data, not as a replacement for established techniques.

In professional settings, firms use AI when refining pricing strategies or incorporating new data sources. Akur8, for instance, applies transparent machine learning to insurance pricing so actuaries can see how rate factors are derived [Source: Akur8]. Earnix offers dynamic pricing tools that adjust in near real-time as new information arrives [Source: Earnix]. These tools operate within existing actuarial frameworks rather than outside them.

Catastrophe modeling provides a different example. Verisk AIR and Moody's RMS use AI-augmented simulations to estimate losses from natural disasters across thousands of scenarios [Source: Verisk AIR] [Source: Moody's RMS]. The underlying logic still rests on actuarial principles. AI extends the reach of those principles to datasets too large or too detailed for manual calibration.

Across these contexts, AI is framed as operating within the mathematical foundations of the field. It appears as an extension of established modeling practices, shaped by the same focus on risk, uncertainty, and disciplined analysis that defines actuarial work.

## What AI Helps With

AI is commonly expected to support how risk is modeled and evaluated over time. Discussions often focus on its ability to work with large and complex datasets. This matters most when estimating future outcomes tied to pricing, reserves, or long-term liabilities.

In reserving, actuaries compare methods to determine how much capital an insurer should hold against future claims. Milliman Arius now integrates AI-augmented techniques that help actuaries test reserve estimates against a wider set of assumptions [Source: Milliman Arius]. WTW has developed similar tools, using machine learning to flag anomalies in development triangles that might otherwise go unnoticed [Source: WTW]. These tools do not replace actuarial judgment. They surface patterns that warrant closer examination.

Fraud detection is a growing area of adoption. Shift Technology applies AI to claims data to identify suspicious patterns early in the review process [Source: Shift Technology]. The models learn from historical fraud cases but still rely on human investigators to confirm findings and decide next steps.

The Society of Actuaries and Casualty Actuarial Society have both published research framing AI as a modeling tool rather than an autonomous decision-maker [Source: SOA] [Source: CAS]. The SOA's 2024 generative AI primer and the CAS's 2022 E-Forum on machine learning reflect a profession actively defining how these techniques fit within existing practice.

For students, these developments shift how modeling work is understood. Actuarial analysis is no longer limited to producing a single result from a fixed set of assumptions. It often involves comparing models and stress-testing outcomes. Coursework in regression, time series, and simulation connects directly to the techniques firms now augment with AI.

## Where AI Falls Short

A common misunderstanding is assuming AI can stand in for the assumptions and judgment that underpin risk models. AI can process large datasets and surface patterns. Actuarial analysis depends on decisions about how models are structured and what assumptions are appropriate. These choices shape results long before any computation takes place.

More complex models do not automatically produce better estimates. In actuarial work, models also need to be interpretable and testable. The American Academy of Actuaries has emphasized that actuaries remain responsible for understanding and explaining the models they use, regardless of how those models are built [Source: American Academy of Actuaries]. When complexity increases, tracing why a model behaves a certain way becomes harder to do reliably.

There is also a tendency to overestimate how well AI responds to changing risk environments. Actuarial models often rely on historical data reflecting past conditions rather than future uncertainty. The NAIC's model bulletin on AI in insurance highlights the need for ongoing monitoring and governance when insurers adopt AI-driven tools [Source: NAIC]. When underlying patterns shift or data quality degrades, AI-supported models may behave unpredictably.

Deloitte's research on actuarial pricing notes that firms adopting AI still depend heavily on validation workflows and human oversight [Source: Deloitte]. In practice, AI is rarely viewed as an independent solution. Actuarial work continues to rely on validation, governance, and ongoing professional review.

## What to Keep in Mind

For actuarial science students, AI is usually discussed through the lens of uncertainty. Actuarial work is built around estimating outcomes that cannot be known in advance. AI enters the conversation where models explore how risk changes under different assumptions.

Control matters throughout. Actuarial models are expected to be tested, explained, and defended. This is especially true when they inform pricing or long-term financial commitments. As models grow more complex, tracing why results change or how individual assumptions affect outcomes becomes harder.

The SOA and CAS both integrate predictive modeling into their exam syllabi and continuing education requirements [Source: SOA] [Source: CAS]. Students preparing for professional exams or internships will encounter these expectations directly. Familiarity with AI techniques is increasingly expected. So is the ability to evaluate when those techniques are appropriate and when simpler methods suffice.

AI is presented as a tool that works within the discipline's existing responsibilities. It sits alongside assumptions, validation, and professional judgment, not above them.
