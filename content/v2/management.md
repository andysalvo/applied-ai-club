# AI in Management

## Where AI Appears

**Workforce analytics and talent management.** Organizations use platforms like Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, and Visier to analyze workforce data -- turnover patterns, hiring outcomes, engagement scores, and performance metrics. These systems apply machine learning to identify which factors predict employee retention, which teams are at risk of attrition, and where hiring processes produce the strongest outcomes. The data informs decisions that managers previously made on intuition alone. [Source: Workday; Visier; Deloitte Human Capital Trends]

**Hiring and recruitment.** LinkedIn's Recruiter platform uses AI to match candidates to roles based on skills, experience, and behavioral signals. HireVue applies machine learning to assess video interviews, analyzing response content and communication patterns. Pymetrics (now part of Harver) uses neuroscience-based games and AI to evaluate candidates for cognitive and behavioral traits. These tools are widespread in corporate recruiting, and they raise significant questions about fairness and bias. [Source: LinkedIn; HireVue; Harver]

**Performance management and evaluation.** Companies like Lattice, 15Five, and BetterWorks incorporate AI into continuous performance management platforms. These tools analyze feedback patterns, goal completion rates, and peer evaluations to help managers identify high performers, development opportunities, and potential issues. Some organizations use AI-generated nudges to prompt managers to give feedback, recognize contributions, or check in with team members. [Source: Lattice; 15Five]

**Organizational decision support.** McKinsey's research on AI in management describes how organizations use data-driven systems to support strategic decisions -- resource allocation, market entry, organizational restructuring. Palantir's Foundry platform and similar enterprise tools integrate data from across the organization to give leadership a comprehensive view of operations. The intent is more informed decisions, not automated ones. [Source: McKinsey Global Institute]

**Process automation and operational efficiency.** UiPath, Automation Anywhere, and similar robotic process automation (RPA) platforms use AI to automate routine administrative tasks -- expense approvals, scheduling, report generation, compliance documentation. For managers, this means less time on administrative overhead and more time on judgment-intensive work. Gartner estimates that by 2025, half of all management tasks involving administrative workflows will be at least partially automated. [Source: UiPath; Gartner]

## What AI Helps With

**Making workforce patterns visible.** AI surfaces patterns in employee data that are difficult to detect manually. Visier's analytics platform can identify, for example, that employees in a specific role who do not receive a promotion within 18 months have a significantly higher turnover rate. This kind of insight lets managers intervene before problems become visible in exit interviews. [Source: Visier; Deloitte]

**Reducing bias in structured decisions.** When hiring criteria are clearly defined and consistently applied, AI can reduce some forms of human bias -- like inconsistent screening standards across recruiters or the halo effect in interviews. The operative word is "can." Poorly designed AI systems can also amplify bias if the training data reflects historical patterns of discrimination. The tool is only as fair as its design. [Source: Harvard Business Review]

**Scaling management practices.** As organizations grow, maintaining consistent management practices across hundreds of teams becomes difficult. AI-powered platforms help standardize processes -- goal-setting, feedback cadences, performance calibration -- while surfacing where specific teams or managers may need additional support. This is not about replacing managers. It is about giving them better information. [Source: Lattice; McKinsey]

**Freeing time from administrative work.** RPA tools handle the repetitive work that consumes a significant portion of managerial time. Automation Anywhere reports that clients typically automate 30-40% of administrative tasks within the first year of deployment. The freed-up time is supposed to go toward strategic thinking, coaching, and relationship-building -- the parts of management that AI cannot do. [Source: Automation Anywhere; UiPath]

## Where AI Falls Short

**Culture, relationships, and morale are not in the data.** The most important aspects of management -- building trust, navigating interpersonal conflict, maintaining team morale, developing people -- resist quantification. AI can track survey scores and engagement metrics, but these are proxies. A team can score well on engagement surveys and still be struggling with dynamics that only a perceptive manager would notice. [Source: Harvard Business Review]

**AI-driven hiring tools have documented bias problems.** Amazon famously scrapped an AI recruiting tool that penalized resumes containing the word "women's." HireVue faced criticism for opaque assessment criteria. Illinois passed legislation requiring disclosure when AI is used in video interview analysis. These are not edge cases -- they reflect a fundamental challenge in using historical data to make decisions about people. [Source: Reuters; Illinois AI Video Interview Act]

**Algorithmic management can erode trust.** When employees perceive that decisions about their careers are being made by algorithms rather than humans, it can damage trust and morale. Research from the Academy of Management has documented that employees respond differently to algorithmic decisions than to human ones, even when the outcomes are identical. How a decision is made matters, not just what is decided. [Source: Academy of Management Journal]

**Complexity does not equal better management.** Adding AI tools to management processes can increase complexity without improving outcomes. If managers do not understand the tools, or if the tools generate recommendations that conflict with organizational context, the result is confusion rather than clarity. Gartner has noted that many AI implementations in management fail because they are deployed without adequate change management. [Source: Gartner]

## What to Keep in Mind

**The ethical stakes are high.** AI in management directly affects people's livelihoods -- who gets hired, who gets promoted, whose performance is flagged. These decisions carry moral weight that technical accuracy alone does not address. Organizations using AI in people-related decisions need clear governance, regular audits, and genuine accountability. [Source: Deloitte; Harvard Business Review]

**Regulation is emerging.** New York City's Local Law 144 requires bias audits for AI-driven hiring tools. The EU AI Act classifies employment-related AI as high-risk, requiring transparency and human oversight. These regulations signal a broader trend: AI in management will face increasing scrutiny, and organizations need to prepare for it. [Source: NYC Local Law 144; EU AI Act]

**Management is fundamentally about people.** AI can inform management decisions with better data. It can automate administrative tasks. It can surface patterns. But the work of management -- motivating people, making hard calls, building culture, developing talent -- remains human. The most effective managers will use AI as one input among many, not as a substitute for the judgment and empathy that leadership requires. [Source: McKinsey Global Institute]

**Students entering management need both analytical and interpersonal skills.** The management curriculum at institutions like Penn State's Smeal College of Business increasingly integrates data analytics alongside organizational behavior and leadership. Students who can work with AI tools and understand organizational dynamics will be best positioned for the evolving role. [Source: Penn State Smeal]
