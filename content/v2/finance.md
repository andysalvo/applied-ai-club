# AI in Finance

## Where AI Appears

Finance deals in uncertainty. Pricing assets, estimating risk, allocating capital -- these tasks depend on processing information under conditions that shift constantly. AI enters where the volume or complexity of that information exceeds what manual methods handle well.

In coursework on investments, corporate finance, or risk management, quantitative analysis already dominates. AI extends that foundation. Portfolio analytics platforms now process thousands of securities against multiple risk factors simultaneously. BlackRock's Aladdin system, for instance, runs risk analytics across more than $21.6 trillion in assets under management, modeling exposures that no team could track manually [Source: BlackRock Aladdin]. Bloomberg Terminal integrates AI-driven analytics that help surface patterns in market data as conditions change [Source: Bloomberg Terminal].

Credit evaluation is another area where AI has taken hold. Lenders process high volumes of applications, each requiring assessment against default risk, income stability, and repayment history. Zest AI applies machine learning to credit underwriting decisions, aiming to evaluate borrower profiles with fewer of the gaps that traditional scorecards leave [Source: Zest AI]. In the loan review process, JPMorgan's COiN platform uses natural language processing to interpret commercial loan agreements, compressing work that once took legal and finance teams thousands of hours [Source: JPMorgan COiN].

Students tend to encounter these tools indirectly. Internships may involve dashboards or analytics platforms shaped by automated models. Case discussions about capital budgeting or forecasting increasingly reference algorithmic screening or data-driven signals. The shift is less about replacing spreadsheets than about expanding what analysts can evaluate in a given timeframe.

## What AI Helps With

Finance expects AI to increase analytical throughput. Running complex models, detecting anomalies in transaction data, generating forecasts across many variables -- these are tasks where speed and scale matter. AI handles repetitive pattern recognition so that analysts can focus on interpretation.

Risk management is a natural fit. Identifying emerging exposures across market, credit, or operational risk requires monitoring many signals at once. NICE Actimize applies AI to anti-money-laundering and fraud detection, scanning transaction flows for patterns that rule-based systems often miss [Source: NICE Actimize]. S&P Global's Kensho platform uses natural language processing to analyze financial events and their market implications, processing unstructured data that would otherwise require manual review [Source: S&P Global Kensho].

A 2025 CFA Institute study found that asset managers increasingly describe AI as a way to maintain consistency across analyses and to expand the number of scenarios evaluated before making allocation decisions [Source: CFA Institute]. The Brookings Institution's 2025 case study on AI in finance noted similar patterns across banking and insurance, where firms use automated models to stress-test portfolios against a wider range of economic conditions [Source: Brookings Institution].

For students studying financial modeling, the practical shift is worth noting. Analysts are less likely to build every model from scratch. They are more likely to interpret outputs, question assumptions, and understand how automated tools fit within a broader decision process. AI is framed as a complement to financial reasoning. It does not replace the need to understand what a model is actually doing.

## Where AI Falls Short

Financial decisions require judgment about risk tolerance, strategic priorities, and regulatory constraints. AI processes data. It does not weigh whether a firm should accept more risk to pursue growth or pull back to protect capital. That distinction matters and tends to get lost in discussions that treat prediction and decision-making as the same thing.

AI-generated forecasts are not inherently more accurate. Model outputs depend on data quality, the assumptions built into the model, and whether historical patterns still apply. Market conditions shift in ways that past data may not represent. The Federal Reserve's SR 11-7 guidance on model risk management exists precisely because financial models -- automated or not -- can produce results that appear precise while lacking reliability [Source: Federal Reserve SR 11-7]. Oversight and validation remain essential.

There is also a tendency to overestimate how well AI adapts to new environments. Financial markets reflect human behavior, regulatory changes, and macroeconomic events. During periods of volatility or structural change, automated systems can struggle when conditions deviate from training data. The 2020 market disruption demonstrated this clearly. Many quantitative models failed to anticipate the speed and nature of pandemic-driven selloffs.

AI can also amplify biases present in historical data. Credit models trained on past lending decisions may replicate patterns of exclusion. The Brookings Institution study highlighted concerns about fairness in automated underwriting, particularly when the data used to train models reflects longstanding disparities [Source: Brookings Institution]. Zest AI has attempted to address this by designing models that reduce demographic bias in credit decisions, though measuring fairness in lending remains an active and unresolved challenge [Source: Zest AI].

Finance relies on interpretation, scenario thinking, and understanding incentives. Automated tools operate within those frameworks. They do not replace them.

## What to Keep in Mind

Finance places significant weight on transparency and accountability. Because decisions affect markets, firms, and stakeholders, understanding how a model works matters as much as what it outputs. The CFA Institute has emphasized that asset managers using AI need clear documentation of model assumptions, data inputs, and performance limitations [Source: CFA Institute].

Regulatory scrutiny shapes how AI is deployed. Financial institutions operate under strict oversight. The Federal Reserve's SR 11-7 framework requires that models -- including those driven by machine learning -- meet standards for validation, documentation, and governance [Source: Federal Reserve SR 11-7]. Firms that adopt AI without accounting for these requirements face both regulatory and operational risk.

There is a persistent tradeoff between speed and interpretability. AI can accelerate analysis considerably. But professionals must still explain their decisions, justify assumptions, and ensure outputs align with strategic and regulatory expectations. A model that produces a recommendation no one can explain to a regulator or a client has limited practical value.

Students entering finance will likely work alongside these tools rather than build them. Understanding what AI does well, where it struggles, and how firms govern its use is more relevant than mastering the underlying algorithms. The field values analysts who can think critically about model outputs and place them in context.
