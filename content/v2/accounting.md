# AI in Accounting

## Where AI Appears

Accounting work often involves reviewing high volumes of transactions and verifying accuracy across records. AI tends to surface in areas where that review work is repetitive and rule-driven. Students may encounter these discussions in courses on auditing, financial reporting, taxation, and internal controls.

In professional settings, AI is frequently mentioned alongside transaction review, risk assessment, and compliance monitoring. Firms like KPMG use platforms such as Clara to analyze full populations of audit-relevant data rather than relying on sampling alone [Source: KPMG Clara]. Deloitte's Omnia platform applies machine learning to flag anomalies in journal entries that might otherwise go unnoticed in manual review [Source: Deloitte Omnia].

AI tends to come up when accounting work scales beyond what a team can review line by line. Reconciliation platforms like BlackLine automate matching across accounts and systems, reducing the hours spent on manual tie-outs [Source: BlackLine]. In tax, tools like Thomson Reuters ONESOURCE use natural language processing to search regulatory guidance across jurisdictions [Source: Thomson Reuters ONESOURCE]. These are discussed as supporting technologies that fit within existing workflows rather than replacing professional judgment.

Accounting students often encounter these ideas when thinking about how work is reviewed at different stages. This includes planning an audit, reconciling accounts, or understanding how controls are tested. AI is usually mentioned in relation to how reviewers decide where to look more closely when time and resources are limited.

The gap between classroom problems and firm-scale practice also shapes where AI appears. In class, datasets are small and contained. At firms, continuous transaction streams flow across many entities and accounts. AI is discussed as part of how firms manage that difference while still meeting documentation and reporting standards [Source: PCAOB].

## What AI Helps With

AI is commonly expected to make accounting work more efficient by handling tasks that involve reviewing large amounts of data. Discussions tend to focus on automating routine checks and applying consistent rules across records.

In practice, these expectations show up in how teams think about repetitive work. Scanning transactions for errors, comparing records across systems, and applying the same checks across many accounts are frequently cited examples. EY's Canvas platform, for instance, structures audit workflows so that standardized procedures run consistently across engagements [Source: EY Canvas]. The emphasis stays on speed and coverage rather than changing how accounting decisions are made.

AI is also expected to expand the scope of what can be examined during audit and assurance work. Rather than testing a small sample, reviewers can look across larger sets of transactions. PwC's Halo platform and its GL.ai tool analyze general ledger data to identify entries that warrant closer attention [Source: PwC Halo/GL.ai]. MindBridge takes a similar approach by assigning risk scores to individual transactions, helping direct reviewer attention toward higher-risk items [Source: MindBridge].

For students, this idea of broader coverage changes how review is imagined. Judgment is still required. But attention can be directed more deliberately toward areas that raise questions. A reviewer might spend less time on routine confirmations and more time investigating the patterns that a risk-scoring tool has surfaced.

## Where AI Falls Short

A common misunderstanding is assuming AI can substitute for professional judgment embedded in accounting standards. AI can process large datasets and apply predefined logic. But accounting work often requires interpretation of materiality and assessment of context. Classification, valuation, and disclosure decisions depend on professional evaluation that cannot be fully automated [Source: AICPA]. The AICPA has issued guidance emphasizing that AI outputs must still be evaluated within existing professional frameworks.

Another oversimplification is the belief that AI-driven analysis is inherently accurate. In accounting, results depend on how data is prepared and how rules are defined. Incomplete records or inconsistent classifications can distort conclusions in audit testing or risk assessment. Without careful oversight, AI-supported analysis may reinforce existing errors rather than surface them. Wolters Kluwer's CCH Axcess platform, used for tax compliance research, still requires practitioners to verify that retrieved guidance applies to the specific facts at hand [Source: Wolters Kluwer CCH Axcess].

Accounting standards also evolve through new guidance and interpretation. The transition to ASC 842 lease accounting standards, for example, required firms to reclassify and remeasure existing arrangements based on updated criteria [Source: FASB ASC 842]. Applying those changes correctly demands awareness of current standards and professional judgment. This limits how independently automated systems can operate.

AI is sometimes discussed as a standalone solution. In practice, accounting work involves documentation, review, and multiple layers of oversight. A recent Journal of Accountancy article noted that even as AI transforms audit procedures, the responsibility for conclusions remains with the signing professional [Source: Journal of Accountancy, Feb 2026]. Existing review processes continue to shape AI's role and limit how much responsibility it can absorb.

## What to Keep in Mind

For accounting students, AI is usually discussed in relation to accuracy and consistency. In courses and case discussions, these ideas come up around financial reporting, audits, and compliance. Small errors in these areas can carry real consequences.

How work is reviewed and explained matters in this field. Accounting emphasizes documentation and clear reasoning. Conclusions need to be supported. This shapes how AI is discussed, since its outputs still need to fit into review processes that require traceability [Source: PCAOB].

Internships at firms increasingly involve exposure to these tools. A student on an audit engagement might see transaction-level risk scores generated by MindBridge or review reconciliations processed through BlackLine. The underlying expectation remains the same: the reviewer, not the tool, is accountable for the conclusion.

AI is part of the systems that support accounting work. It is discussed alongside judgment and oversight, not as a replacement for either.
